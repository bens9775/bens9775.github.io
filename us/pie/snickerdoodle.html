Pie 50: Snickerdoodle

2024-06-15

Recipe from 50 Pies, 50 States by Stacey Mei Yan Fong, p. 75


Taste: 6

Difficulty: 3

Vibes: 5

Suggested pairing: character death

Stacey Fong, the author of this recipe, unilaterally declared the state pie of Connecticut to be the Snickerdoodle based on the current status of the snickerdoodle cookie in the state. A cursory perusal of her book's back-flap bio revealed no authority for such a pronouncement. Now, snickerdoodles--and nutmeg in general--is indeed popular in the Constitution State.

A full text search of the CT state laws archive returns no hits for “snickerdoodle” and I found several web news articles discussing attempts, in different years, to name the snickerdoodle or the oatmeal chocolate chip cookie as the official “state cookie”. No news articles I could find discussed such a bill actually passing. Wikipedia doesn't list any state cookie, pie, or dessert for Connecticut. (Unlike Missouri, which has actually had a real state dessert since 1904: the ice cream cone!) Like most of the best things in life, the legal status of a pie is immaterial to its quality.

I hope the preceding paragraph hasn't led you astray. This pie isn't just a cookie in a crust. It's a legitimate pie, which shares with the cookie an ingredient list: flour, sugar, butter, eggs, baking soda, cream of tartar, cinnamon, and nutmeg. However, the pie filling was much wetter than cookie dough and could easily be spread around the pie crust (which was a pretty typical unbaked single crust).

While snickerdoodle dough balls are rolled in cinnamon sugar, this pie filling was covered with a spiced hot syrup before baking.

As the pie baked, the syrup soaked into the filling and formed a cracked crust reminiscent of the typical cookie appearance.

We served the pie to players of a D&D game. All enjoyed the pie, but the game itself was disastrous--3 out of 5 player characters died.
